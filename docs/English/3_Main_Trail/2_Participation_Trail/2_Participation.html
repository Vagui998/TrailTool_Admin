How do we learn?

In the design phase, you might have already encountered theoretical insights into the transdisciplinary learning process. Let’s now turn these ideas into practices. This part of the TRAIL offers hands-on advice, tools, and reflections on the execution of the learning experience.

Iterative process

As quoted earlier, it is the journey that matters most in transdisciplinarity. This also applies to the way we learn in a project. Transdisciplinary learning is iterative learning. When handling wicked problems, iterative learning is the only way forward. Because of its multifaceted changing nature, you need to regularly revisit the problem and rethink the plan of action over the course of the project.

Transformation

This comes with the risk that the team may not be advancing too much towards a direct solution. You can set some basic output requirements to guide the process towards a solution, but the transformation during the iterative learning process is maybe even more important output – though less clear. Iterative learning is a way of working on the wicked problem at hand, as it transforms all the involved stakeholders and this way the wicked problem itself.

Unpredictable outcomes

The open-ended nature of transdisciplinary projects can be confusing and frustrating to students and other stakeholders alike. Regular courses or work projects offer clear tangible output or requirements, while a transdisciplinary project may seem never-ending. Sub-pages below focus on the way this open-endedness can affect different stakeholders – and how to cope with this.
