How to communicate

Following everything you have learned so far, you can hopefully imagine that the slogan ‘communication is key’ applies strongly to transdisciplinary collaboration. However, this is easier said than done. What do you have to take into account concerning communication? And what tools can you use to smoothen it? This page provides lessons and examples.

Common language

For smooth communication, it is essential to have a mutual understanding of what your team is about to do. In other words, you need a Common Vision, which in turn also requires a Common Language. To establish this, you have to gain insight into your own and each other’s backgrounds. Where the use of some terms might be obvious to you and your peers, people from other disciplines may understand the same words totally differently.

Communication Tools

This part of the TRAIL discusses strategies to develop a common language and vision. The Theory of Change exercise, for example, is found suitable to capture the diversity in a transdisciplinary team. This exercise is used in co-creation processes to create a clear mission plan, to align members around a common vision, to unearth hidden misconceptions, and to analyze how the long-term goals can be reached.

Breaking the walls between silos of all participants disciplines and professions doesn’t happen at once. Really understanding each other requires continuous reflection. That sounds somewhat heavy, but it basically comes down to the fact that you should never forget to pose questions by everything you and others say. Do I understand it rightly? And: are others likely to rightly understand what I want to communicate? Feeling free and safe to speak out in the team is, once again, essential in this aspect. Only this way disciplinary boundaries can be surpassed, and through transdisciplinary knowledge arises.

Verbindend communiceren

For the adventurous readers who want to go more in-depth on this topic: Behind the development of a common language is the mutual understanding of each others beliefs (epistemological and methodological). You can try to go in depth, even if not all team members are yet familiar with the project.
