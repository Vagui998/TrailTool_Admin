    Unravelling Transdisciplinarity

    Transdisciplinarity, despite its varied nuanced definitions (Klein, 2018), involves some consistent elements that make it stand out between other cross-disciplinary forms of collaboration. The first element focuses on the type of collaborating partners: academic and non-academic partners learn, work, and research together in transdisciplinary settings (Klein, 2018; Visscher, 2023). The second element concerns the nature of the addressed problem, emphasizing complex societal issues lacking singular solutions, often referred to as wicked problems (Williams, 2022). The third element revolves around the nature of engagement: different partners learn and work on an equal basis, truly collaboratively generating new knowledge and theory (Visscher, 2023).

    This TRAIL has a special focus on transdisciplinary education, those types of projects of which education is a part, and the educational processes in a transdisciplinary project. However, ‘education’ is definitely regarded as one singular most important stakeholder of the mix. The TRAIL is designed to meet all interests in an equal way. Often, the differences between these interests overlap more than one might think on a first glance, and education is an important change maker for all parties in this society. You find more about the educational perspective of transdisciplinarity on the next page.

    Equality in Transdisciplinary Collaboration

    In transdisciplinary collaboration, partners contribute on an equal basis, creating, learning, working, and researching together to generate new knowledge and theories (Visscher, 2023). Discussions with experts within and beyond Avans University of Applied Science – where this TRAIL tool is developed – underscore the absence of a shared understanding of transdisciplinarity. Particularly in relation to interdisciplinarity, distinctions appear unclear, with transdisciplinarity often characterized as dealing with complexities in societal issues and collaboration with stakeholders.

    Inter-, or transdisciplinary: a common confusion.

    Academic literature and dialogues with experts, underscore the absence of a shared understanding of transdisciplinarity. Particularly the distinction with interdisciplinary collaboration, is often unclear. Where both approaches strive towards the integration of different types of knowledge, transdisciplinarity distinguishes itself by the involvement of non-academic stakeholders as equal participants in the same project. In transdisciplinarity, the diversity of perspectives and ‘types of knowledge’ are higher, and the problem at hand tends to be more complex. As the complexity increases, so do the challenges and learning opportunities in transdisciplinary collaborations increase as well. That is why there is TRAIL.
